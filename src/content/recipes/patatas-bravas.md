## Chef's Note

The double-cook method is what separates bar-standard patatas bravas from unforgettable ones. Parboiling the potatoes first roughens the edges and gelatinizes the starch, creating a shatteringly crisp exterior when they hit the hot oil. Make the bravas sauce in advance -- it benefits from resting as the smokiness from the pimenton rounds out. Cut the potatoes into irregular chunks, not uniform cubes; the jagged edges crisp up better.

## Directions

1.  **Make the Bravas Sauce:** Heat 2 tbsp olive oil in a medium saucepan over medium heat. Add the diced onion and cook for 5-6 minutes until softened. Add the garlic and cook 30 seconds. Stir in the smoked paprika, sweet paprika, and cayenne. Cook for 30 seconds until fragrant. Add the crushed tomatoes, sugar, and salt. Simmer for 15-20 minutes until thickened. Stir in the sherry vinegar. For a smooth sauce, blend with an immersion blender or pass through a sieve. Set aside.

2.  **Make the Alioli:** Whisk together the mayonnaise, grated garlic, and lemon juice. Season with a pinch of salt. Refrigerate until serving.

3.  **Parboil the Potatoes:** Place the potato chunks in a large pot of generously salted cold water. Bring to a boil and cook for 8-10 minutes until the edges are tender but the centers still have some resistance. Drain well and spread on a sheet pan to steam dry for 10 minutes. This step is crucial for crispiness.

4.  **Fry the Potatoes:** Heat 4 cups of oil in a large heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven to 375F. Working in batches to avoid crowding, fry the potatoes for 5-7 minutes, turning occasionally, until deeply golden and crispy. Transfer to a wire rack set over a sheet pan. Season immediately with flaky sea salt.

5.  **Serve:** Pile the hot potatoes on a warm serving platter. Drizzle generously with the bravas sauce and dot with the alioli. Serve immediately with toothpicks or small forks -- this is tapas, after all.
