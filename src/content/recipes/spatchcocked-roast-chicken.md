## Chef's Note

Spatchcocking (splitting out the backbone and flattening the bird) is the single best way to roast chicken at home. The flat chicken cooks 25-30% faster than a whole bird, the thighs reach proper doneness without the breasts drying out, and the entire surface gets exposed to heat for maximum browning. This technique comes from the tradition of breaking down chickens for grilling in southern Europe and was popularized for home ovens by America's Test Kitchen after testing dozens of methods. Kenji López-Alt's research proved that a 4-5 lb chicken spatchcocked, roasted at 475°F, and rested properly produces chicken that's crispy outside and juicy inside every single time. The key is an instant-read thermometer: 165°F at the thickest part of the thigh (without touching bone), and you're done.

## Directions

### Prep (Can do this up to 8 hours ahead)

1. **Remove the Backbone:** Place the whole chicken on a cutting board, breast-side up. Using sharp kitchen shears or a knife, cut along both sides of the backbone (the side closest to you and the far side). Remove the backbone completely. (Don't throw it away  -  save for stock.) You now have a butterfly chicken.

2. **Flatten:** Flip the chicken skin-side up. Press down firmly on the breastbone with the heel of your hand. You'll hear a crack  -  this is the breastbone breaking and flattening the bird. The chicken should now lie completely flat.

3. **Pat Dry:** Use paper towels to pat the chicken completely dry, especially the skin. Moisture is the enemy of browning.

4. **Season Generously:** In a small bowl, combine salt, pepper, minced garlic, fresh herbs, smoked paprika, and 2 tbsp olive oil to form a paste. Rub this all over the chicken  -  under the skin (gently separate the skin from the breast meat with your fingers and tuck seasoning underneath), on the legs, and everywhere on top. You want bold seasoning.

5. **Rest (Optional but helpful):** If you have time, let the seasoned chicken sit uncovered on the counter for 30-60 minutes (or up to 8 hours refrigerated, uncovered, so the skin dries further). This allows the seasoning to penetrate and the skin to dry, which leads to better browning.

### Roasting

6. **Preheat:** Heat your oven to 475°F. Make sure the oven rack is positioned in the lower-middle portion (not the top  -  you want heat from below, not above). If using a baking stone or steel, place it on the middle oven rack now.

7. **Prepare the Pan:** Oil a large rimmed baking sheet (or a roasting pan) with 1 tbsp olive oil. Pour 1/2 cup water or stock into the bottom of the pan (this prevents the drippings from smoking and helps create a pan sauce later).

8. **Place the Chicken:** Put the spatchcocked chicken, skin-side up, directly on the oiled baking sheet. Make sure it's centered and that there's space around the edges for air to circulate.

9. **Roast:** Put the baking sheet in the preheated oven. Roast for 35-45 minutes, depending on the size of the bird. A 4-lb chicken is done in about 35-40 minutes; a 5-lb bird needs closer to 45 minutes.

10. **Check for Doneness:** Using an instant-read thermometer, check the temperature at the thickest part of the thigh (the part closest to where the thigh meets the body), making sure the thermometer doesn't touch bone. It should read 165°F. If it's not there yet, check every 2-3 minutes. The breasts should also register 160-165°F, but they'll cook faster, so focus on the thighs.

11. **The Visual Cue:** The skin should be deep golden brown, almost mahogany in color. If the skin is pale but the temperature is 165°F, you've cooked it correctly (don't cook it longer for color  -  the chicken will dry out).

### Rest and Finish

12. **Rest:** Remove the chicken from the oven and transfer to a cutting board (with a lip to catch juices). Let it rest, uncovered, for 10 minutes minimum. This allows the juices to redistribute throughout the meat. If you cut immediately, all the juice runs out onto the board. Resting ensures every bite stays moist.

13. **Pan Sauce (Optional but worthwhile):** While the chicken rests, place the roasting pan on the stovetop over medium-high heat. Scrape up all the browned bits from the bottom (the fond is pure flavor). Let simmer for 2-3 minutes until slightly reduced. Taste and season with salt and pepper. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve if you prefer a smooth sauce, or serve as-is.

14. **Carve:** Cut the chicken into four pieces: two legs (thigh + drumstick together) and two breasts. Or carve further into individual thighs, drumsticks, and breast halves if preferred.

15. **Serve:** Arrange on a platter, pour any pan sauce over the top, and serve with fresh lemon wedges and a finishing pinch of fleur de sel. The crispy skin, juicy meat, and bright lemon are all you need.

### Pro Tips for Perfect Results

- **Thermometer is essential:** Don't rely on leg wiggling or juice color. Use an instant-read thermometer  -  it removes all guesswork.
- **Don't overcook:** The moment the thighs hit 165°F, the chicken is done. Cooking to 170°F or higher results in dry meat. Resting time brings carry-over cooking.
- **Use the backbone:** Save it for stock. A good chicken stock requires the backbone, so don't waste it.
- **Skin texture:** If your oven has a convection setting, you can use it for 10-15 minutes at the end (separate from regular roast) to really crisp the skin. Watch carefully so it doesn't burn.
- **Timing confidence:** 475°F, spatchcocked, 35-45 minutes (depending on size), thermometer-verified at 165°F, then 10-minute rest = perfect chicken, every time.

### For Make-Ahead Meal Prep

- Roast chicken ahead and refrigerate up to 4 days
- Use shredded meat for grain bowls, salads, tacos, and soups
- Make stock from the carcass and bones
- One roasted chicken easily feeds a family of 4 for one meal, or can be stretched across multiple meals throughout the week

### The Science

Spatchcocking works because:
- **Even thickness:** A flattened chicken has uniform thickness, so everything cooks at the same rate
- **Surface area:** The flat shape exposes more skin to direct heat, enabling better browning
- **Thigh vs. breast balance:** Thighs and breasts are now at similar distances from the heat source, so they finish at approximately the same time (rather than needing the breasts to reach 160°F while thighs finish at 165-175°F)
- **High temperature + short time:** 475°F for 35-45 minutes creates a Maillard reaction on the skin (browning) before the meat overcooks
