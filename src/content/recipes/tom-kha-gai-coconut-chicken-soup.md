## Chef's Note

Tom kha gai is one of Thailand's most comforting soups - a fragrant, silky broth made by simmering chicken in coconut milk with aromatic lemongrass, galangal, and lime leaves, then balancing it with fish sauce, palm sugar, and a squeeze of lime juice. The soup comes together in 20 minutes, tastes like you've been cooking for hours, and every component has a purpose: the galangal brings peppery warmth, the lemongrass adds citrus perfume, the lime leaves add a floral bitterness that prevents the soup from being one-note. Thai home cooks know that this balance - salty, sweet, sour, and spicy in harmony - is what makes Thai food Thai. Even if you skip the galangal and lime leaves (they can be hard to find), the soup works.

## Directions

1. **Prepare the Broth Base:** Heat the coconut milk and chicken stock together in a medium saucepan over medium heat. Don't boil - a gentle simmer is all you need. The coconut milk should blend smoothly with the stock.

2. **Add the Aromatics:** Once the liquid is hot, add the dried red chilies (left whole), galangal slices, lemongrass pieces, and kaffir lime leaves. Bring to a gentle simmer and let the aromatics infuse for 3-4 minutes. The kitchen should smell deeply fragrant - peppery, citrusy, and herbal all at once.

3. **Season the Broth:** Add the fish sauce and palm sugar. Stir gently to combine. The broth should taste aggressively salty and sweet at this point - the balance will come with the lime juice added at the end.

4. **Add the Chicken:** Add the chicken pieces in a single layer. Gently stir to separate the pieces. Bring back to a gentle simmer and cook for 4-5 minutes until the chicken is just cooked through (no pink). Don't overcook or it will become tough.

5. **Add the Vegetables:** Add the mushrooms and cherry tomatoes. Simmer for 2-3 minutes until the mushrooms are tender and the tomatoes are just cooked. If using whole green chilies, add them now (they'll stay whole and guests can push them aside if they want less heat).

6. **Balance with Lime Juice:** Remove from heat. Add the fresh lime juice, tasting as you go. Start with 3 tbsp and add more if needed. The soup should taste balanced - not aggressively salty, not aggressively sour, but both flavors present and bright. This is the crucial moment. Adjust fish sauce and palm sugar one last time if needed.

7. **Taste and Final Adjustments:** The soup should hit all four Thai flavor notes:
   - Salty: from fish sauce (should not taste fishy, but deeply savory)
   - Sweet: from palm sugar and coconut milk (should not be cloying)
   - Sour: from lime juice (should be bright and forward)
   - Spicy: from the chilies (should warm the back of your throat without overwhelming)

8. **Strain (Optional):** For a refined presentation, strain the broth into a clean saucepan, reserving the chicken and mushrooms, then return them to the strained broth. This removes the lemongrass and lime leaves so they don't interfere with eating. For a more casual version, leave everything in.

9. **Serve:** Ladle into bowls, dividing the chicken and vegetables evenly. Drizzle with coconut milk if desired (a small spoonful of extra cream floated on top is beautiful). Garnish with fresh cilantro, Thai basil, and serve with lime wedges for additional brightness.

**Galangal Substitution:** Galangal tastes peppery and floral - unlike ginger. Ginger can substitute in a pinch (use the same amount) but the flavor will be different and less authentic.

**Lemongrass Sourcing:** Essential for authentic flavor. Available frozen, fresh, or even jarred at most Asian markets. If unavailable, use 1 tbsp of lemon zest (not the same but adds citrus brightness).

**Kaffir Lime Leaves:** These add a floral, slightly bitter note that's irreplaceable. Available fresh or frozen at Asian markets. If truly unavailable, use the zest of one regular lime as a brightness substitute.

**Fish Sauce Amount:** 3 tbsp seems like a lot, but it's balanced by sweetness and lime. Taste first before adding the final tbsp - some brands are stronger than others.

**Protein Options:** Shrimp works beautifully (add for the last 3-4 minutes), or tofu for vegetarian (add at the vegetable stage). Chicken is most traditional.

**Heat Level:** The whole dried chilies stay whole so guests can avoid them if desired. If you want the soup spicy, break one or two of the chilies before adding. Thai bird chilies are very hot - adjust to your family's tolerance.

**Make-Ahead:** Tom kha gai keeps for 3-4 days in the refrigerator. The flavors deepen overnight. Reheat gently - don't boil or the coconut milk may separate. Add a squeeze of fresh lime before serving.
