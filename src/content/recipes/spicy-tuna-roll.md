## Chef's Note

The spicy tuna roll was invented in the 1980s at Japanese restaurants in Los Angeles, making it a purely Japanese-American creation. It was born from practicality  -  using the scraps and trimmings left over from slicing tuna for sashimi, minced and mixed with spicy mayo. Kewpie mayo (made with only egg yolks, not whole eggs) gives a richer, tangier base than American mayo, and a single drop of sesame oil adds fragrance without overwhelming the fish.

## Directions

1. **The Tuna:** Mince the tuna with a sharp knife into small, rough pieces (not a paste  -  you want texture). Fold in the mayo, sriracha, sesame oil, and scallions. Taste and adjust heat. Keep cold until ready to roll.
2. **Prep Your Station:** Lay the bamboo rolling mat on a clean surface. Have your tezu bowl, nori sheets, rice, and filling within reach.
3. **Lay the Nori:** Place a half-sheet of nori, rough side up, on the mat. Dip your hands in tezu, then spread a thin, even layer of rice across the nori, leaving a 1-inch strip bare at the top edge.
4. **The Dam:** Build a small ridge of rice along the top edge of your rice layer. This acts as a dam to catch the filling so it doesn't slide out during rolling.
5. **Fill:** Lay a line of spicy tuna across the center of the rice.
6. **Roll:** Lift the bottom edge of the mat and roll it over the filling, tucking tightly. Continue rolling forward in one smooth motion. Squeeze gently to seal.
7. **Cut:** Wet a sharp knife with tezu. Cut the roll in half, then cut each half into 3-4 pieces. Wipe and re-wet the knife between cuts for clean slices.
