## Chef's Note

Gohan is the foundation of every Japanese meal  -  properly cooked rice where each grain is distinct yet slightly sticky, holding together just enough to pick up with chopsticks. The ratio matters: 1 part rice to 1.25 parts water is the standard. Short-grain rice has higher starch content than long-grain, which creates that signature stickiness. The water ratio is finicky  -  too much and you get gluey porridge, too little and you get hard grains. A rice cooker takes the guesswork out, but a heavy-bottomed pot with a tight-fitting lid works equally well.

## Directions

1. **Rinse the Rice:** Place the rice in a large bowl and fill with cold water. Swish with your hand, then pour off the cloudy water. Repeat 4-5 times, using fresh water each time, until the water runs mostly clear. This removes excess starch that would otherwise make the rice gummy. Do not over-rinse or you'll lose the starch needed for proper stickiness. The water should be slightly cloudy, not crystal clear.

2. **Drain:** Pour the rinsed rice into a fine-mesh strainer and let drain for 5 minutes. This prevents excess water from diluting the liquid you'll add.

3. **Combine:** Transfer the drained rice to a heavy-bottomed pot with a tight-fitting lid (or rice cooker). Add 2.5 cups of cold water. Let the rice soak for 10 minutes  -  this allows the grains to absorb water evenly and cook more uniformly.

4. **Bring to a Boil (Stovetop Method):** Place the pot over high heat, uncovered. Listen for the water to come to a boil  -  you'll hear the sound of boiling before you see much activity. This takes 5-7 minutes.

5. **Reduce Heat and Cover:** Once boiling, immediately reduce heat to the lowest setting and cover with the tight-fitting lid. Do not lift the lid for any reason  -  even a peek releases steam and disrupts cooking.

6. **Cook Low and Slow:** Simmer on the lowest heat for 15 minutes. The rice will continue to absorb water and soften.

7. **Remove from Heat:** Turn off the heat. Keep the lid on and let the rice rest for 10 minutes. This allows carryover steaming to finish cooking any remaining hard centers, and the grains will set up slightly.

8. **Rice Cooker Method (Alternative):** If using a rice cooker, simply add the soaked rice and water to the cooker, close the lid, and press the button. Most rice cookers handle Japanese short-grain rice perfectly  -  they're designed for exactly this.

9. **Fluff:** Remove the lid carefully (watch for steam). Using a wooden rice paddle or fork, gently fluff the rice, running the paddle through from top to bottom to release steam and separate the grains slightly. Do not stir aggressively  -  you want to maintain the texture.

10. **Serve:** Serve immediately while still warm. Each grain should be slightly soft, slightly sticky, and distinct  -  not mushy or hard.

11. **Keep Warm:** To keep rice warm, cover with the lid and a kitchen towel for up to 2 hours. After that, refrigerate.

12. **Reheat:** Leftover rice reheats best in a rice cooker's warming function, or in the microwave with a damp paper towel covering the bowl to restore moisture.

**The Water Ratio Rule:** This is the single most critical element. 1 cup rice : 1.25 cups water. Measure carefully. Too much water = gluey. Too little = hard centers. A kitchen scale is your friend here.
