## Chef's Note

Dan dan noodles are about the sauce that coats every strand, not a soupy broth. The sauce is assembled raw in the bottom of each bowl -- the hot noodle cooking water emulsifies it into a silky, clinging paste. Chinese sesame paste (made from toasted sesame) has a deeper, nuttier flavor than tahini, but tahini works if you toast it briefly in a dry pan first. Yacai (preserved mustard greens) provides a funky, salty crunch in the pork topping that is difficult to substitute, but it can be found in most Asian grocery stores.

## Directions

1.  **Prepare the Sauce Base:** Divide the following among four serving bowls: sesame paste, soy sauce, Chinkiang vinegar, chili oil, ground Sichuan peppercorn, grated garlic, and sugar. Do not stir yet -- the hot noodle water will bring it together.

2.  **Cook the Pork Topping:** Heat the neutral oil in a wok or skillet over high heat. Add the ground pork and cook for 3-4 minutes, breaking it into very small, crispy crumbles. When browned, add the Shaoxing wine and minced yacai. Stir-fry for 1-2 minutes until the pork is deeply savory and slightly caramelized. Set aside.

3.  **Cook the Noodles:** Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. If using bok choy, blanch it for 60 seconds, remove with a spider, and divide among the bowls. Cook the noodles according to package directions until just al dente. Before draining, ladle 3-4 tbsp of the starchy cooking water into each bowl.

4.  **Emulsify the Sauce:** Using chopsticks, vigorously stir the cooking water into the sauce base in each bowl until the sesame paste dissolves and the sauce becomes creamy and emulsified.

5.  **Assemble:** Drain the noodles and divide among the bowls. Toss with the sauce until every strand is coated. Top with the crispy pork and yacai mixture.

6.  **Garnish:** Scatter with sliced green onions and chopped peanuts. Drizzle with additional chili oil to taste. Serve immediately -- these do not wait well.
