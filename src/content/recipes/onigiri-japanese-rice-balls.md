## Chef's Note

Onigiri are Japanese rice balls - simple, transportable, and a staple of bentos and home cooking for centuries. What makes them special is the ritual: the rice is still warm when shaped, and the small amount of salt on your hands seasons the outside just enough. You can fill them with whatever you like (pickled plume, tuna mayo, salmon, sesame), wrap nori around the outside, and pack them for school, work, or a picnic. They're best when still warm, but they hold well at room temperature for 4-5 hours. A squeeze of warm rice in your palm wrapped by a sheet of nori is one of the most comforting things to eat.

## Directions

1. **Cook the Rice:** If starting from scratch, prepare 1 cup Japanese short-grain rice according to package directions (usually 1:1 ratio with water). Let rest for 10 minutes after cooking. Use warm rice for the best texture when shaping.

2. **Prepare the Work Surface:** Have a small bowl of water nearby. Wet your hands slightly and sprinkle a pinch of salt into your palm. This is crucial - it prevents sticking and adds a subtle salty flavor to the outer rice.

3. **Form the Triangle (Basic Shape):** Take about 1/2 cup cooked rice in your palm. Make a small indent in the center with your thumb, creating a pocket about the size of a marble. Place 1 tbsp of your chosen filling into the pocket.

4. **Encase the Filling:** Cup your hand to gently bring rice up and around the filling, enclosing it completely. You should have a ball of rice with filling inside. Squeeze gently but firmly - you want it compact enough to hold together but not so tight that it becomes dense.

5. **Shape into Triangle:** Place the rice ball on your work surface. Using both hands (re-wet and re-salt if needed), gently shape it into a triangle by applying even pressure on three sides. The shape comes from your hands, not from pressing into a mold. A triangular onigiri is iconic and practical - it sits flat and is easy to wrap.

6. **Wrap with Nori:** Cut nori into strips about 1 inch wide and 3-4 inches long. Wrap one strip around the middle of the onigiri, like a belt. The nori will stick to the rice from its slight moisture. If wrapping right before serving, the nori stays crispy; if you wrap earlier, it softens slightly from the rice moisture.

7. **Garnish and Store:** Sprinkle with sesame seeds if desired. Serve warm or at room temperature. Onigiri can be made ahead and stored in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 5 hours, or frozen for up to 1 month (thaw at room temperature before serving).

**Filling Ideas:** Keep fillings minimal - a single flavor is more traditional. Tuna mayo, umeboshi (pickled plum), grilled salmon, kombu (kelp), or just sesame salt all work beautifully.

**Hand Temperature:** Warm hands make shaping easier and prevent the rice from cooling too much. If your hands get cold, re-wet with warm water and re-salt.

**Nori Texture:** For maximum crispness, wrap nori just before serving. For softer nori, wrap 30 minutes ahead and let the moisture from the rice soften it slightly.
