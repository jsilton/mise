## Chef's Note

Crispy shallots (hành phi in Vietnamese, hom jiao in Thai) are a cornerstone condiment across Southeast Asia, adding texture, sweetness, and aroma to soups, salads, and stir-fries. The secret to perfect shallots is the **cold-oil start**. Starting shallots in cold oil allows their moisture to gently evaporate as the oil heats up, resulting in a shatteringly crisp texture. Hot-oil starts burn the outer layers while leaving the interiors soggy. Save the strained shallot oil; it is an incredibly flavorful base for searing proteins or frying eggs.

## Directions

1. **Slicing:** Peel the shallots. Slice them into thin, uniform rings (about 1/16-inch thick) using a mandoline or a very sharp chef's knife. Uniformity is crucial for even browning.
2. **Cold Start:** Place the sliced shallots in a cold, small saucepan. Pour in the neutral oil until the shallots are just submerged.
3. **Slow Fry:** Turn the heat to medium. Once the oil begins to gently bubble around the shallots, reduce the heat to medium-low. Cook for 15 to 20 minutes, stirring occasionally to ensure they do not clump or burn.
4. **The Pull:** Watch the shallots closely towards the end. The moment they turn a pale, light golden brown, immediately pour the contents of the pan through a fine-mesh strainer set over a heat-proof bowl. Do not wait for them to look dark brown in the oil, as carryover heat will continue to cook and darken them as they cool.
5. **Drain and Salt:** Spread the hot shallots in a single layer on a paper towel-lined plate. Immediately sprinkle with a pinch of kosher salt while they are still wet with oil. This helps them dry crisp and seasons them.
6. **Store:** Allow them to cool completely. Store in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. Use the leftover shallot-infused oil in the fridge for cooking.
