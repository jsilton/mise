## Chef's Note

Mapo tofu is defined by its ma la -- the numbing tingle of Sichuan peppercorn combined with the deep, complex heat of doubanjiang. The biggest mistake home cooks make is using silken tofu, which disintegrates. Medium-firm holds its shape while still being custardy inside. Parcooking the tofu in salted water firms it up and seasons it through. The doubanjiang must be fried in oil until the oil turns red -- this step is called "chao" and is the foundation of the dish's color and flavor.

## Directions

1.  **Parcook the Tofu:** Bring a medium saucepan of salted water to a gentle boil. Carefully slide in the tofu cubes and simmer for 2-3 minutes. This firms the tofu and removes any raw bean flavor. Drain very gently and set aside.

2.  **Cook the Pork:** Heat the neutral oil in a wok or large skillet over high heat. Add the ground pork and cook for 3-4 minutes, breaking it into small crumbles, until browned and the fat has rendered.

3.  **Fry the Doubanjiang:** Push the pork to the side. Add the doubanjiang and fermented black beans to the oil in the wok. Stir-fry for 1-2 minutes until the oil turns a deep red and the paste is very fragrant. This is the critical step.

4.  **Build the Sauce:** Add the garlic, ginger, and green onion whites. Stir-fry for 30 seconds. Pour in the chicken broth, soy sauce, and sugar. Bring to a simmer.

5.  **Add the Tofu:** Gently slide the parcocked tofu into the sauce. Do not stir aggressively. Instead, gently push the tofu around with the back of a ladle or by shaking the wok. Simmer for 5 minutes to let the tofu absorb the sauce.

6.  **Thicken:** Give the cornstarch slurry a stir and drizzle it into the wok in two additions, gently swirling between each. The sauce should coat the tofu in a glossy, clinging glaze.

7.  **Finish:** Drizzle with sesame oil and chili oil. Sprinkle generously with the ground Sichuan peppercorn and green onion greens. Serve immediately over steamed white rice.
